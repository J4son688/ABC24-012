About Mumbai Bites

Rooted in tradition. Inspired by flavor. Powered by people.

Our Story

Mumbai Bites was born from a love of bold spices, hearty meals, and joyful gatherings. What started as a humble kitchen experiment is now a local gem bringing authentic Indian street food to the heart of Gaborone.

We mix Mumbai’s energetic street flavors with a modern café vibe — creating a place where everyone feels welcome.

Meet the Team

Chef Arjun

With over 15 years in professional kitchens, Chef Arjun brings both tradition and technique to Mumbai Bites. Born and raised in Pune, India, his culinary journey started at his grandfather’s roadside dhaba, where he learned to make rotis by hand before he could even reach the counter. That early passion turned into a profession when he earned a diploma from the Institute of Hotel Management in Mumbai.

Arjun has trained under master chefs across India and worked in five-star hotels before deciding to shift his focus to authentic regional cuisine. What sets him apart isn't just his mastery of spices, but his deep respect for the **roots of every recipe**. Whether it’s a simple dal tadka or a complex biryani, he believes every dish tells a story.

At Mumbai Bites, Chef Arjun is more than the head of the kitchen — he’s the soul behind the flavor. From hand-picking produce at local markets to blending his own garam masala mix, he brings precision and passion to every plate. He also runs cooking classes and food storytelling nights, making sure every guest not only eats, but connects.

Neha Patel

Founder and visionary behind Mumbai Bites. Storyteller, spice lover, and kitchen soul.

Neha Patel didn’t grow up in a kitchen — she grew up in the middle of Mumbai’s bustling Dadar market, where food wasn’t just survival, it was the heartbeat of the city. Her family ran a small spice stall there, and from the age of nine, Neha was grinding masalas by hand, tasting everything, and learning how each pinch could transform a dish into something magical.

She was never formally trained as a chef — her school was the streets. Her teachers were the chaatwalas, dosa vendors, vada pav aunties, and her mother, who ruled the home kitchen like a queen.

After finishing university, Neha moved to Botswana with her husband. Gaborone was quiet in comparison — warm and kind, but missing something. She couldn’t find the taste of home. No tangy pani puri, no fiery butter chicken, no comforting masala chai.

One night in 2022, she hosted a small dinner at her apartment. She made pav bhaji, paneer tikka, and mango lassi. Friends invited friends. Soon, she was cooking for 30 people every Friday. Word spread — not just about the food, but about how eating with Neha felt like visiting home.

That’s when she knew: Botswana didn’t just need Indian food. It needed Indian soul.
